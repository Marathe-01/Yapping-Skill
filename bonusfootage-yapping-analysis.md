## What is "Yapping"?

Tom Boyd has essentially branded and codified the "yap" format — talking directly to camera in a raw, unpolished style that *looks* casual but is actually well thought out. His core thesis: **the rawness is the aesthetic, not the process.**

---

## Core Principles Extracted

### 1. Lived Experience > Generic Advice
Yap content is built on personal, specific, messy details that only YOU could know. "I just left a meeting where my boss said this exact thing" beats generic advice every time. This is what makes yap content ChatGPT-proof — AI can't replicate your lived moments.

### 2. The "So What?" Test
After you say your point out loud, ask "so what?" If the answer is "obviously" or "duh," you have a truism, not a point. **Real points have tension. Someone could disagree.**

- BAD: "Hustle culture is toxic" (everyone agrees — no tension)
- GOOD: "Hard work is the excuse successful people use to avoid talking about luck and timing" (that's a point — it creates friction)

### 3. Build for Shares, Not Views
People share when they think:
- "Finally someone said it"
- "My friend needs to hear this"
- "This confirms something I was thinking but didn't know how to articulate"

### 4. Talk to ONE Person, Not Everyone
When you try to talk to a lot of people, you end up talking to no one. Think about the exact person you want the viewer to share this video with. Ask: What do they look like? Where do they hang out? What problems are they solving? Hyper-specific topics connect with many people because that one person wants to share it.

### 5. Volume is the Strategy
Content is a volume game. Stop overthinking every video. The biggest advantage is not overthinking. Put the camera on your face and just go. You learn patterns, delivery, and flow through reps — not through planning the perfect video.

### 6. One Clear Point Per Video
When starting out, focus on one clear point. Don't try to sound smart. Try to be understood. Each video should feel like one continuous thought with momentum — you're building towards something.

## Tactical Tips (From His "5 Tips for Yapping" Post)

1. **Study small creators (<10K)** and their outlier videos — not just big creators
2. **Create a swipe folder** of favorite yap videos; study the first 3 seconds and text hooks
3. **The "I Believe" test** — can someone watch your video and know exactly what you believe?
4. **Change the language** on videos you study (subtitles in another language) so you can ignore the words and focus purely on tonality, delivery, and cadence
5. **Disregard advice that slows you down** — the goal is volume so you can see data and find patterns

---

## Hook Strategy

- **Verbal hooks matter most.** Use prompts, templates, talk to yourself — find what rolls off your tongue and sounds real and YOU.
- Tap into your raw energy. Stop regurgitating what is "technically good" — everyone else is doing that and audiences are desensitized.
- Choose the hook that feels truest and most natural when you say it out loud.
- Use text hooks to fill in gaps if you can't hit everything in the verbal hook.

## Key Takeaways for Your Own Yapping

1. **Start with lived experience** — what happened to you today that made you feel something? That's your content.
2. **Apply the "So What?" test** to every point before recording. If no one could disagree, dig deeper.
3. **Optimize for shares** by making content so specific that the viewer immediately thinks of someone to send it to.
4. **Don't polish** — momentum and authenticity beat production value. One take mentality.
5. **Study scripts obsessively** — pull apart other creators' videos to understand pacing, structure, and hooks.
6. **Keep a notes tab on your phone** — jot down moments, reactions, things that made you feel something. That's your content bank.
7. **Commit to one strategy for 30-60 days** before adding complexity. Stop consuming competing advice.
8. **Volume reveals patterns** — you can't optimize what you haven't created. Ship fast, learn from data.
